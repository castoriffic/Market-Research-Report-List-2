<p><p>Partial Knee Arthroplasty, also known as unicompartmental knee arthroplasty (UKA), is a surgical procedure designed to treat knee arthritis by replacing only the damaged portion of the knee joint. Unlike total knee replacement surgery, which involves replacing all three compartments of the knee joint, partial knee arthroplasty focuses on the damaged part, preserving healthy tissues and bone. This procedure is ideal for patients with arthritis limited to one compartment of the knee, providing a less invasive and quicker recovery option.</p><p>The global partial knee arthroplasty market is projected to witness significant growth in the coming years. This can be attributed to several factors such as the rising prevalence of knee-related disorders, an increasing geriatric population, advancements in surgical techniques and materials, and a growing demand for minimally invasive procedures. <p><p>Partial Knee Arthroplasty is a surgical procedure to replace a damaged part of the knee joint with an implant. There are two main types of implants: Fixed Bearing and Mobile Bearing. Fixed Bearing refers to an implant where the plastic meniscus is fixed in place, providing stability. In contrast, Mobile Bearing implants allow for slight movement of the plastic meniscus, mimicking the natural knee joint. Both types have their advantages and disadvantages, and the choice depends on the patient's condition and the surgeon's preference.</p></p>
